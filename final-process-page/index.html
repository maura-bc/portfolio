BILT Credit Card Website

A promotional website prototype designed to introduce users to the BILT Credit Card, the only card that earns rewards on rent and mortgage payments.

Role User Interface Designer
Tools Figma
Deliverables Project Proposal · Prototype
Duration 2 months

Overview

I was asked to design an informational website prototype that teaches users about something new, while also showing my understanding of UI design principles. The goal was to create something that feels easy to navigate and simple to understand.

I chose the BILT Credit Card because it is a unique financial product that lets users earn rewards on rent and mortgage payments. The site is structured across five pages:

Project Goals

For this project, I wanted the site to feel clean and modern, with a premium look that still felt readable and easy to use. I also focused on applying design principles in a way that felt natural, while making sure the content was easy for users to scan and remember.

Process

Project Proposal

I started by creating a project proposal that explained how I planned to use different UI design principles throughout the site. For each principle, I described what it is and how I wanted to apply it in the design. The proposal included concepts like Miller's Law, Chunking, the Von Restorff Effect, and the Serial Position Effect.

User Interface Principles

Von Restorff Effect

The Von Restorff Effect is the idea that when similar items are shown together, the one that looks different stands out the most and is more likely to be remembered.

On the homepage, I used this idea in the benefits section by placing five cards in a grid. Four of them use the same dark styling, while the Earn on Rent card is highlighted in gold. Since earning on rent is the feature that makes BILT different from other credit cards, I wanted that card to catch the user's attention first and be the thing they remember most.

Gold card
Styled differently from the other four cards to draw attention and be remembered
Chunking

Chunking is the process of breaking information into smaller, related groups so it feels easier to understand and less overwhelming.

The BILT Card comes with a lot of information, including point rates, card tiers, redemption options, and eligibility details. Instead of placing everything on one page, I spread the content across five pages so users only see what they need in that moment. Within each page, I also grouped related content into smaller sections and cards. On the Features page, for example, the earning process is broken into four step cards, which makes it much easier to follow.

Step cards
Each step is its own card so users process one piece of information at a time
Fitts's Law

Fitts's Law says that the time it takes to click something depends on its size and distance. Larger targets are easier and faster for users to interact with.

I applied this on the homepage by making the Apply Now button large, easy to notice, and simple to click. It stands out clearly against the dark background, so users do not have to search for it. I also kept it in a consistent position across the site, which helps users know where to find it as they move from page to page.

Large Apply Now button
Big target size means users can click it quickly without needing to be precise

Final Prototype

The final result is a five-page promotional website for the BILT Credit Card. It takes users from learning about the card's benefits, to comparing it with other options, to applying, while consistently using the UI principles described above.

Retrospective

After finishing this project, I realized that UI principles work better when they support each other instead of being treated as separate ideas. Combining the Von Restorff Effect, the Serial Position Effect, and Chunking helped me make the information feel easier to take in and easier to remember.

I also learned that it is okay to change a design decision when it stops making sense. Early on, I tried forcing certain principles into the layout in ways that felt unnatural, and going back to fix that made the final design much stronger.

Working on a financial product also reminded me how important trust is in design. Every choice, from the color palette to the layout, needed to make the site feel reliable, clear, and easy to use.
